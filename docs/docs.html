Section 01

Introduction

This project implements a complete physics-constrained minimum-lap-time optimiser for a Formula 1-style vehicle on real-world circuits (Silverstone, Monza, Spa, and 22 others).

The framework simultaneously optimises the racing line (lateral offset from the centerline) and the speed profile, while enforcing track-boundary limits and a realistic tyre friction ellipse derived from aerodynamic downforce, engine power, and mechanical grip.

All components are differentiable end-to-end via JAX, enabling exact gradient-based optimisation with L-BFGS through a progressive penalty strategy.

Section 02

Fourier Track Modelling

Why Fourier Series?

Fourier series are well-suited for closed-loop circuits because they are:

  • Globally smooth and infinitely differentiable
  • Periodic by construction — perfect loop closure with zero extra constraints
  • Spectrally convergent — high accuracy with relatively few coefficients
  • Analytically differentiable to any order via closed-form derivative matrices
  • Naturally suppress high-frequency noise present in raw GPS data

Compared to piecewise splines or dense pointwise optimisation, spectral parametrisation reduces the number of optimisation variables by a factor of 5–20 while guaranteeing $C^\infty$ smoothness.

Fourier Representation

A scalar periodic function on $[0,2\pi]$ is approximated as:

$$f(t) = c_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{N} \bigl( c_{2k-1}\cos(kt) + c_{2k}\sin(kt) \bigr)$$

In matrix form, $f(\mathbf{t}) = \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{t})\,\mathbf{c}$, where $\mathbf{A}$ is the design matrix of size $(M \times 2N+1)$ evaluated at $M$ collocation points.

Derivative matrices of order $n$ follow directly from trigonometric identities. For order 1:

$$\mathbf{D}^{(1)}_{k} : \quad \cos(kt) \;\to\; -k\sin(kt), \quad \sin(kt) \;\to\; k\cos(kt)$$

The same Fourier basis is applied to: centerline coordinates $x_c(t),\,y_c(t)$; track half-widths $w_R(t),\,w_L(t)$; lateral offset $d(t)$; and speed profile $v(t)$. The resolution schedule $N \in \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 48, 64, 96, 128, 192\}$ provides a natural multigrid warm-start strategy.

Section 03

Frenet-Serret Frame

The raw centerline is fitted after arc-length reparametrisation:

$$t = 2\pi\frac{s}{L}, \qquad s = \text{cumulative arc length}$$

The Frenet frame is then computed analytically from the Fourier derivatives:

$$g_c = \left\|\dot{\mathbf{r}}_c\right\| = \sqrt{\dot{x}_c^2 + \dot{y}_c^2}$$
$$\mathbf{T} = \frac{1}{g_c}\!\begin{pmatrix}\dot{x}_c\\\dot{y}_c\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{N} = \begin{pmatrix}-T_y\\T_x\end{pmatrix}$$
$$\kappa_c = \frac{\dot{x}_c\,\ddot{y}_c - \dot{y}_c\,\ddot{x}_c}{g_c^3}$$

Higher-order derivatives $\dot{\mathbf{N}}$, $\ddot{\mathbf{N}}$ are also computed analytically and precomputed as constant arrays. These are required for the product-rule expansion of racing-path curvature.

Section 04

Racing Line Parametrisation

The racing line is expressed as a lateral deviation from the centerline in the Frenet normal direction:

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_c(t) + d(t)\,\mathbf{N}(t)$$

with $d(t) = \mathbf{A}_d(t)\,\mathbf{c}_d$ being the Fourier-parametrised lateral offset. This turns track boundaries into simple scalar inequalities on $d$:

$$-w_L(t) \leq d(t) \leq w_R(t)$$

First and second derivatives of the racing path are expanded via the product rule. For the first derivative:

$$\dot{x}_r = \dot{x}_c + \dot{d}\,N_x + d\,\dot{N}_x, \qquad \dot{y}_r = \dot{y}_c + \dot{d}\,N_y + d\,\dot{N}_y$$

And the second derivative:

$$\ddot{x}_r = \ddot{x}_c + \ddot{d}\,N_x + 2\dot{d}\,\dot{N}_x + d\,\ddot{N}_x$$

The curvature of the racing path is then:

$$\kappa_r = \frac{\dot{x}_r\,\ddot{y}_r - \dot{y}_r\,\ddot{x}_r}{g_r^3}, \qquad g_r = \sqrt{\dot{x}_r^2+\dot{y}_r^2}$$
Section 05

Speed Profile & Kinematics

The parameter $t \in [0, 2\pi]$ is a spectral coordinate, not physical time. The speed $v(t) = \mathbf{A}_v(t)\,\mathbf{c}_v$ is optimised directly in this space.

The infinitesimal physical time element is:

$$d\tau = \frac{g_r(t)}{v(t)}\,dt$$

Hence the total lap time — the objective to minimise — is:

$$T_\text{lap} = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{g_r(t)}{v(t)}\,dt \;\approx\; \sum_{i=1}^{M} \frac{g_r(t_i)}{v(t_i)}\,\Delta t$$

Longitudinal and lateral accelerations in physical time follow from the chain rule:

$$a_\text{lon} = \frac{dv}{d\tau} = \frac{v}{g_r}\frac{dv}{dt}, \qquad a_\text{lat} = v^2\kappa_r$$
Section 06

Vehicle Physics Model

The model is calibrated to a $750\,\text{kg}$, $\approx\!1000\,\text{hp}$ Formula 1 car. All limits grow with speed due to aerodynamic downforce terms $C_{d,f}v^2$ and $C_{d,b}v^2$.

Longitudinal Limits

Forward (power-limited at high speed, grip-limited at low speed):

$$a_\text{front}(v) = \frac{1}{m}\min\!\left(F_{g,f} + C_{d,f}v^2,\;\frac{P}{v}\right) - \frac{Dv^2}{m}$$

Braking (rear-biased aerodynamics):

$$a_\text{brake}(v) = \frac{F_{g,b} + C_{d,b}v^2 + Dv^2}{m}$$

Lateral Limit

$$a_\text{side}(v) = 1.2 \times \frac{F_{g,b} + C_{d,b}v^2}{m}$$

Combined Grip — Friction Ellipse

$$\left(\frac{a_\text{lon}}{a_\text{lon,max}(v)}\right)^{\!2} + \left(\frac{a_\text{lat}}{a_\text{side}(v)}\right)^{\!2} \leq 1$$

The right-hand side limit $a_\text{lon,max}$ is $a_\text{front}$ when accelerating and $a_\text{brake}$ when decelerating, chosen via a differentiable $\text{where}$ conditional in JAX.

Section 07

Optimisation Strategy

Problem Formulation

The decision variables are the Fourier coefficients for the lateral offset and speed:

$$\mathbf{p} = \begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{c}_d \\ \mathbf{c}_v\end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{2(2N+1)}$$

The full constrained problem is:

$$\min_{\mathbf{p}}\; T_\text{lap}(\mathbf{p}) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \begin{cases} \left(\frac{a_\text{lon}}{a_\text{lon,max}}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{a_\text{lat}}{a_\text{side}}\right)^2 \leq 1 \\ -w_L(t) \leq d(t) \leq w_R(t) \\ v(t) \geq v_\text{min} \end{cases}$$

Progressive Penalty Method

Since jaxopt.ScipyMinimize is unconstrained-only, nonlinear inequality constraints are handled via a quadratic penalty:

$$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{p},\mu) = T_\text{lap}(\mathbf{p}) + \mu \sum_i \Bigl[\text{ReLU}\bigl(c_i(\mathbf{p})\bigr)\Bigr]^2$$

A progressive schedule $\mu = 10^0, 10^1, \dots, 10^8$ is used. Each stage warm-starts from the previous solution. Final constraint violation routinely reaches $< 10^{-8}$ — effectively machine precision for the physics model.

WHY NOT SLSQP?  jaxopt provides no general nonlinear-constraint solver. ProjectedGradient requires a closed-form projection (unavailable for the speed-dependent friction ellipse); OSQP is QP-only. The penalty method with L-BFGS and JAX autodiff is the correct choice.

Multigrid Warm-Start

The resolution schedule $N \in \{1,2,4,8,\dots,192\}$ forms a multigrid continuation strategy. Coefficients are zero-padded when moving to the next level:

$$\mathbf{c}_{N_\text{new}} = \begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{c}_{N_\text{prev}} \\ \mathbf{0}\end{bmatrix}$$

Coarse levels find the global basin; fine levels add geometric detail. This avoids poor local minima that pure high-resolution optimisation would encounter.

JAX Implementation

# All inner functions are JIT-compiled
@jit
def forward(p):          # → (d, v, g_r, κ, a_lon, a_lat)
    ...

@jit
def lap_time_fn(p):      # objective
    return jnp.sum(g_r / jnp.maximum(v_r, 1e-3)) * dt

@jit
def cviol_fn(p):         # squared constraint violation
    return jnp.sum(relu(ellipse)**2 + relu(track)**2 + ...) * dt

# Exact gradient via autodiff — no finite differences
vag = jit(value_and_grad(lambda p: lap_time_fn(p) + mu * cviol_fn(p)))

solver = ScipyMinimize(method='L-BFGS-B', fun=vag, value_and_grad=True)
p = solver.run(p).params
Section 08

Implementation

  • Track fitting (arc-length reparametrisation, Fourier lstsq) runs once in NumPy — cheap
  • All optimisation maths runs in JAX with float64 precision (jax_enable_x64=True)
  • Fourier design and derivative matrices are precomputed constant arrays pushed to device once
  • JIT compilation traces on first call; all subsequent optimisation iterations run at full speed
  • Adaptive Fourier order per track: $N_\text{FIT} = \min(192,\, \max(32,\, N_\text{pts}/3))$ — ensures numerical stability of the least-squares fit
  • Per-track JSON output (~230 KB each) stores 600 decimated points per resolution level
  • 26 circuits processed automatically from CSV; the web viewer loads data lazily on demand
Section 09

Results

On Silverstone the optimiser converges from 238 s (N=1, constant speed on the centerline) down to approximately 109 s at N=192 — compared to Hamilton's 2020 pole of 87.1 s. The ~22 s gap is explained by:

  • Quasi-static physics (no transient tyre load, no suspension)
  • Conservative friction constraint satisfaction — penalty method is softer than hard SLSQP constraints
  • No slipstream, DRS, or multi-lap energy management
  • The spectral parametrisation smooths aggressive late-apex geometry

The speed profile correctly shows low speed in slow corners and peak speeds (~310 km/h) on the Hanger Straight and Maggotts–Becketts complex. Lateral G reaches ~4.5 g in fast corners — physically plausible for a 2020-spec car.

Future extensions can include tyre load sensitivity, suspension dynamics, energy management (hybrid systems), and multi-lap stint optimisation.
